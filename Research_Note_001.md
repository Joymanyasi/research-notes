# Research Note 001: The Invisible Patient
**Genomic Representation, AI Governance, and Breast Cancer Prediction in East Africa**

**Joy Manyasi Kabaka**  
*Genomics and Biotechnology Working Group, Africa Bioethics Network | Founder, Sequence Africa*

---

## Executive Summary
Artificial intelligence is reshaping cancer care through diagnostic imaging, genomic risk prediction, and precision medicine. Yet these systems are only as equitable as the data used to build them. Across biomedical AI, African populations remain critically underrepresented in training datasets, raising concerns about the fairness, reliability, and clinical relevance of technologies increasingly promoted as solutions to Africa's cancer burden.

This research note argues that the absence of East African women from biomedical datasets is not merely a technical oversight but a **social and political condition** requiring social and political analysis. Questions of algorithmic fairness cannot be separated from questions of representation, governance, and scientific sovereignty.

---

## Conceptual Framework
My investigation treats algorithmic inequity not as a failure of technology alone, but as the downstream consequence of historical and institutional decisions:

**The Inequity Pipeline:**
1. Underrepresentation in Genomic Research
2. Underrepresentation in Biomedical Datasets
3. AI Models Trained on Incomplete Populations
4. Reduced Reliability for East African Patients
5. Diagnostic and Prediction Disparities
6. **Need for Data Justice, AI Governance, and Scientific Sovereignty**

---

## The Problem
Africa contains more than **70% of global human genetic diversity** yet accounts for only **2% of participants** in genome-wide association studies. Only 1% of global health data originates from African countries. A 2025 scientometric analysis of 264 AI mammography studies found zero datasets from low-income countries.

When populations are missing from training data, the consequences extend beyond exclusion. AI systems may systematically underperform, contributing to delayed diagnosis and unequal access to the benefits of precision medicine.

### Why East Africa and Why TNBC?
Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) provides a critical lens for this investigation. It is the most aggressive subtype, with higher prevalence in East African populations than in European cohorts. Emerging genomic studies reveal **distinct biological characteristics** in East African patients that are rarely reflected in the datasets used to develop contemporary AI systems.

---

## Research Questions
1. **Structural:** What historical and political-economic factors contribute to underrepresentation?
2. **Technical-Social:** How does dataset imbalance influence AI performance and fairness for East African populations?
3. **Governance:** How do existing frameworks enable or constrain equitable AI development?
4. **Normative:** What alternative approaches (including **Ubuntu-informed** frameworks) can support context-sensitive AI?

---

## Why This Matters
Underrepresentation is not simply a data problem; it is a **governance problem** and a **justice problem**. Moving beyond technical optimization requires a focus on **scientific sovereignty**. The future of equitable biomedical AI depends not only on better algorithms, but on whose knowledge, biology, and experiences are included in their design.
